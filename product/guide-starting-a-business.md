## What This Guide Is

Starting a business while keeping your W-2 job is one of the most financially complex situations you can be in. You are simultaneously an employee, a self-employed business owner, a taxpayer in two modes, and a person trying to build a bridge between two very different income streams.

This guide covers everything you need to set up correctly from day one — taxes, retirement, banking, legal structure, insurance — and how to plan the transition from W-2 income to business income without destroying your financial foundation in the process.

## Part 1: The Financial Foundation

### Business banking — separate everything from day one

Open a dedicated business checking account immediately. Do not comingle personal and business finances.

Why this matters:
- **Taxes:** Commingled accounts make expense tracking a nightmare. Business deductions require clear documentation.
- **Legal protection:** Commingling can pierce the corporate veil — meaning your personal assets lose the liability protection your LLC provides.
- **Clarity:** You cannot know if your business is profitable if you cannot see its cash flow separately.

**Mercury** is an excellent choice for early-stage SaaS businesses. No monthly fees, solid API integration, and built for startups. If you already have this set up, you're ahead of most.

### The business credit card

Once your Mercury account is open, apply for a business credit card. Use it exclusively for business expenses. Pay it in full monthly.

Benefits:
- Automatic expense categorization
- Points/rewards on business spending
- Clear paper trail for deductions
- Keeps personal credit card untouched for personal expenses

Recommended for early-stage: Chase Ink Business Cash (no annual fee, 5% on office supplies and internet) or American Express Blue Business Cash.

### What counts as a business expense

Deductible business expenses reduce your self-employment income — and therefore your self-employment tax (15.3%) and income tax. This is real money.

**Common SaaS business deductions:**
- Software subscriptions (Supabase, Vercel, Anthropic API, Resend, etc.)
- Domain and hosting costs
- Legal and professional fees (attorney, accountant, trademark)
- Marketing and advertising
- Home office deduction (if you have a dedicated workspace)
- Equipment (computer, monitor, peripherals — prorated for business use percentage)
- Professional development (courses, books, conferences)
- Business banking and payment processing fees
- Health insurance premiums (if you eventually leave your W-2 and pay your own premiums)

**The home office deduction:**
If you have a room or defined space used regularly and exclusively for business, you can deduct either:
- **Simplified method:** $5 per square foot, up to 300 sq ft = max $1,500/year
- **Regular method:** Calculate the percentage of your home used for business × actual home expenses (rent, utilities, insurance)

Track your square footage and keep receipts. This deduction is legitimate and often under-used.

## Part 2: The Tax Reality of Self-Employment

### Self-employment tax — the number that surprises everyone

When you work for an employer, FICA taxes (Social Security + Medicare) are split: you pay 7.65% and your employer pays 7.65%.

When you are self-employed, you pay both halves: **15.3% self-employment tax on net business income.**

This is on top of your regular income tax.

**Example:** $30,000 in net business income in year one:
- Self-employment tax: $30,000 × 15.3% = $4,590
- Plus income tax (at your marginal rate, likely 22-24%): $6,600-7,200
- Total tax on that $30,000: approximately $11,190-11,790
- Effective tax rate on business income: ~37-39%

The good news: half of SE tax is deductible on your personal return. And every legitimate business deduction reduces the base before this calculation.

**The lesson:** Set aside approximately 30-35% of every dollar of net business income for taxes. This is not optional.

### Quarterly estimated taxes

Once your business generates meaningful income, you are required to pay estimated taxes quarterly — not annually.

**Due dates:**
- Q1 (January 1 - March 31): Due April 15
- Q2 (April 1 - May 31): Due June 15
- Q3 (June 1 - August 31): Due September 15
- Q4 (September 1 - December 31): Due January 15 of next year

**When do you start?** The IRS requires estimated payments when you expect to owe $1,000 or more in taxes for the year from self-employment. That threshold is crossed at approximately $5,000-6,000 in net business income.

**How much to pay?** Use the safe harbor method: pay at least 100% of last year's tax liability (or 110% if your AGI was over $150,000) spread across 4 payments. This protects you from underpayment penalties even if your business grows significantly.

Penalty for not paying estimated taxes: typically 5-8% of the underpaid amount. Avoidable with proper planning.

**Set up IRS Direct Pay:** Pay at irs.gov/payments. Set calendar reminders for all four due dates. This is not automatic — you must initiate each payment.

### Tennessee tax considerations

Tennessee has no state income tax on wages or salary. However:
- **Business income from Krovos LLC** is subject to the Tennessee Franchise and Excise Tax once revenue begins.
- **Minimum franchise/excise tax:** $100/year, due April 15 of the following year.
- As revenue grows, your F&E tax will increase proportionally. Track this and set aside the appropriate amount.

## Part 3: The Solo 401k — Your Most Powerful Tool

### Why the Solo 401k is the best retirement account for a self-employed person

The Solo 401k (also called Individual 401k or Self-Employed 401k) is only available to self-employed individuals with no employees other than a spouse. It has the highest contribution limits of any retirement account available to you.

**2026 contribution limits:**
- **Employee contribution:** Up to $23,500 (same as a regular 401k)
- **Employer contribution (profit-sharing):** Up to 25% of net self-employment income
- **Combined maximum:** $70,000

This means at $100,000 in net business income, you could contribute:
- $23,500 as the "employee" (you)
- $25,000 as the "employer" (also you, as business profit-sharing)
- Total: $48,500 — all tax-deductible

At $200,000 in net business income, you could hit the $70,000 maximum.

**Compare this to a regular IRA:** Maximum $7,000/year. The Solo 401k is dramatically more powerful.

### The December 31 deadline — this is not flexible

You must **establish** your Solo 401k before December 31 of the tax year for which you want to make contributions.

You do not need to fund it by December 31. You can make the actual contributions up to your tax filing deadline (April 15 or October 15 with extension). But the account must **exist** before December 31.

If you miss December 31, you lose the ability to make Solo 401k contributions for that entire tax year. You cannot retroactively create the account.

**Do this now if you haven't already.** Even if you have zero revenue, establish the account. It costs nothing to open and nothing to maintain. Fidelity and Schwab both offer free Solo 401k accounts.

**Fidelity Solo 401k:** fidelity.com/self-employed-401k
**Schwab Solo 401k:** schwab.com/small-business-retirement-plans/individual-401k-plan

### What you need to open a Solo 401k
- Your EIN (already obtained)
- Your business legal name (Krovos LLC)
- Business address
- Your Social Security Number
- Approximately 20 minutes online

### The Roth vs. Traditional decision for your Solo 401k

Most Solo 401k plans offer both Traditional (pre-tax) and Roth (after-tax) options.

**Traditional:** Contributions reduce your taxable income today. You pay taxes when you withdraw in retirement.

**Roth:** Contributions do not reduce taxable income today. Growth and withdrawals in retirement are tax-free.

**For a pre-revenue or low-revenue early-stage business:** Roth is often the better choice. Your taxable income from the business is currently low or zero, so the tax deduction from Traditional is less valuable. Tax-free growth on decades of Roth contributions is extremely valuable.

As your business income grows and pushes you into higher brackets, the traditional deduction becomes more valuable.

## Part 4: Planning the W-2 Exit

### The bridge timeline

You are targeting a W-2 exit in approximately 4 years (by 2030). This is a specific, achievable goal — but it requires a specific, achievable revenue target.

**The key question:** What does Krovos LLC need to generate in annual revenue for you to confidently leave your W-2?

To answer this, work backward from your current financial picture:

1. **What does your household need monthly?** (Current expenses minus what Ethan's income covers)
2. **What will health insurance cost if it's no longer through your employer?** (Until Ethan's retirement, health insurance continues through his employer — this is a significant advantage)
3. **What income do you need from Krovos to feel secure?**
4. **Add a 20-30% buffer** for tax obligations and business expenses

For most W-2 employees leaving for self-employment, a general rule: your business needs to generate 1.5x your W-2 salary in gross revenue to net the same take-home, accounting for self-employment tax and business expenses.

**The staged approach:**
- Year 1: Product built, first paying customers, $15-30K revenue
- Year 2: Growth, $50-80K revenue, covering most personal expenses
- Year 3: $80-120K revenue, W-2 departure becomes realistic
- Year 4: $120K+ revenue, W-2 exit confirmed

These are illustrative targets, not guarantees. Your actual numbers will depend on Krovos's pricing, churn, and growth rate.

### What to do with Krovos revenue before you exit the W-2

While you have W-2 income covering your household needs, every dollar of Krovos revenue is surplus. Use it strategically:

1. **Build 6 months of business operating expenses** as a business emergency fund before drawing a salary
2. **Maximize Solo 401k** contributions — this reduces your SE tax and builds retirement assets
3. **Pay quarterly estimated taxes** on Krovos income
4. **Reinvest in growth** — marketing, tools, and any paid resources that accelerate Krovos revenue
5. **Begin drawing a modest salary** once the business is consistently profitable — this normalizes income and simplifies tax planning

### Health insurance planning for the exit

Currently: health insurance through your employer. Ethan's firefighter benefits cover the family through his employment (until he retires at 56).

When you leave your W-2:
- COBRA: Continue your current employer plan for up to 18 months. Expensive — you pay the full premium (employer + employee share), typically $600-1,200/month for family coverage.
- Ethan's plan: Confirm whether you can be added to Ethan's firefighter plan when you leave your employer. This is likely the cleanest option.
- Marketplace plan: Available if you don't qualify for Ethan's plan. As a self-employed person, health insurance premiums are deductible as a business expense.

**Action item:** Before leaving your W-2, confirm with Ethan's HR department whether you can join his plan upon losing your employer coverage. If yes, your COBRA period or marketplace gap disappears entirely.

### The one thing most people get wrong about leaving a W-2

They leave before the business is ready.

The pressure to "make it work" after leaving a stable income can lead to poor decisions — underpricing, taking wrong clients, burning out, or compromising the product.

The advantage of your situation: Ethan's income provides a safety net. You don't need Krovos to be profitable on day one of your exit. But you do need a clear revenue milestone that tells you the business is ready to support your life.

Define that milestone now. Write it down. When Krovos hits it consistently for 3+ months, you will know it is time.
